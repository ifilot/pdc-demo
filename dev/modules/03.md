# Module 3: Mathematical Modelling Principles

## Overview

Process control depends on being able to describe how a process changes with time. A mathematical model gives that description. It helps us predict transient behavior, compare equipment choices, estimate the effect of disturbances, and identify which physical parameters matter most for control.

In chemical engineering, the most useful dynamic models are often built from fundamental principles such as conservation of mass and energy, together with constitutive relationships for reaction, heat transfer, and flow. These models do more than match data. They connect physical design and operating conditions to dynamic response.

The central idea of this module is simple: modelling is not just equation writing. It is a structured engineering task. We define the goal, choose the system boundary, state assumptions, write balances, solve the model, interpret the result, and check whether the model is accurate enough for the intended use.

Another key theme is that many real process models are nonlinear. Exact nonlinear models can be realistic but difficult to analyze. For that reason, engineers often derive linear approximations around a steady operating point. These linearized models reveal quantities such as steady-state gain and time constant, which are essential in process dynamics and control design.

## Why Fundamental Models Matter

A fundamental model is based on physical laws. That matters for three reasons.

First, the same small set of conservation principles applies to many systems. Tanks, heat exchangers, reactors, and piping networks all rely on the same balance framework.

Second, a first-principles model often remains useful when conditions change. If flow rate, volume, temperature, or operating point shifts, the model can usually be re-evaluated without starting from scratch.

Third, and most important for control, the model explains why a process behaves the way it does. Volume affects accumulation. Flow affects residence time. Reaction rate affects both steady state and speed of response. Heat-transfer parameters affect how quickly temperature changes. A good model links these physical features to observed dynamics.

## A Practical Modelling Procedure

A reliable modelling workflow can be summarized in six steps:

1. Define the goal.
2. Prepare process information.
3. Formulate the model.
4. Choose a solution method.
5. Analyze the results.
6. Validate the model.

These steps are listed in order, but real modelling is iterative. If results look unrealistic, the assumptions, data, or even the goal may need to be revisited.

### 1. Define the Goal

The goal determines what kind of model is needed. A model built to estimate whether a response takes seconds or hours is different from a model built to size a safety margin or design a controller.

Useful goals usually answer questions such as:

- What numerical value is needed?
- Which variables influence the response most?
- Is the response monotonic, oscillatory, fast, or slow?
- How accurate must the result be?

If the required accuracy is high, a very simple model may be inadequate. If only rough insight is needed, a compact first-order model may be enough.

### 2. Prepare Information

Before writing equations, define the system clearly. Sketch the process, identify the system boundary, list inputs and outputs, and collect the needed data.

This is also where assumptions are stated. Common examples include:

- well mixed
- constant density
- constant volume
- negligible heat loss
- constant physical properties

These assumptions make the model easier to solve, but they also limit where it can be trusted.

### 3. Formulate the Model

Most dynamic process models combine:

- conservation balances
- constitutive equations
- algebraic simplification

The balances describe accumulation, input, output, generation, and consumption. Constitutive equations describe how variables are related physically, such as reaction rate laws, heat-transfer expressions, or valve flow equations.

After writing the equations, it is good practice to check degrees of freedom. The number of independent equations should match the number of unknowns. If not, the model is incomplete or inconsistent.

### 4. Choose a Solution Method

Some models can be solved analytically. This is especially helpful for simple first-order linear systems, because the resulting equation directly shows the form of the response.

More realistic models are often nonlinear and require numerical solution. Analytical solutions give more insight, but numerical methods are often necessary when the physics is more complex.

### 5. Analyze the Results

Solving the equations is not the end of the job. The result must be interpreted.

Good questions to ask include:

- Does the solution satisfy the initial condition?
- Does it approach the expected steady state?
- Is the sign of the response physically reasonable?
- Are there oscillations, extrema, or time delays?
- Which parameters control the speed and magnitude of the response?

Sensitivity analysis is also important. If small uncertainty in a parameter causes a large change in the answer, that uncertainty matters in design and operation.

### 6. Validate the Model

Validation means checking whether the model is good enough for its intended use. In practice, this usually means comparing predictions with measured data.

No model is ever proven universally true. At best, it is shown to be acceptable over a certain range of conditions. That is enough for engineering, provided the limits of the model are understood.

## Worked Example 1: A Stirred-Tank Mixer

Consider a perfectly mixed tank of constant volume `V` with inlet and outlet flow rate `F`. The inlet concentration changes suddenly from `C_{A0,1}` to `C_{A0,2}`. We want the concentration in the tank, `C_A(t)`.

### Step 1: Write the balance

For component A:

```text
accumulation = in - out
```

which gives

```text
V dC_A/dt = F C_A0 - F C_A
```

or

```text
dC_A/dt + (F/V) C_A = (F/V) C_A0
```

This is a first-order linear differential equation.

### Step 2: Identify the time constant

The standard first-order form is

```text
tau dy/dt + y = K x
```

so here

```text
tau = V/F
K = 1
```

That tells us two things immediately:

- larger volume means slower response
- larger flow rate means faster response

### Step 3: Write the step-response form

For a step in inlet concentration,

```text
C_A(t) = C_A(final) + [C_A(initial) - C_A(final)] e^(-t/tau)
```

If `V = 2.1 m^3` and `F = 0.085 m^3/min`, then

```text
tau = 2.1 / 0.085 = 24.7 min
```

So after one time constant, the concentration has completed about 63.2% of its total change. After about `4 tau`, the tank is very close to its new steady state.

### What this example teaches

This simple model introduces one of the most important ideas in process dynamics: many processes behave approximately like first-order systems, and their speed is captured by the time constant.

## Worked Example 2: An Isothermal CSTR

Now consider a well-mixed isothermal reactor with a first-order reaction:

```text
A -> products,   r_A = -k C_A
```

The component balance becomes

```text
V dC_A/dt = F C_A0 - F C_A - V k C_A
```

or

```text
dC_A/dt + (F/V + k) C_A = (F/V) C_A0
```

This still has first-order form, but now the reaction changes both the steady-state gain and the time constant.

### Time constant

```text
tau = 1 / (F/V + k) = V / (F + V k)
```

Compared with the mixer, the reactor responds faster because reaction removes A in addition to outlet flow.

### Steady-state gain for inlet concentration changes

At steady state:

```text
0 = F C_A0 - (F + V k) C_A
```

so

```text
C_A = [F / (F + V k)] C_A0
```

Therefore

```text
K = F / (F + V k)
```

Since `K < 1`, the reactor concentration changes less than the inlet concentration because reaction damps the effect of the disturbance.

### What this example teaches

Two systems with similar hardware can have quite different dynamics once reaction is included. The model shows not just that the response changes, but exactly why it changes.

## Worked Example 3: Linearizing a Nonlinear Model

Suppose a reactor has second-order kinetics:

```text
V dC_A/dt = F(C_A0 - C_A) - V k C_A^2
```

This model is nonlinear because of the `C_A^2` term. A common control-engineering step is to linearize it around a steady operating point `C_As`.

### Step 1: Expand the nonlinear term

Using a first-order Taylor approximation around `C_As`:

```text
C_A^2 ≈ C_As^2 + 2 C_As (C_A - C_As)
```

### Step 2: Use deviation variables

Define

```text
C_A' = C_A - C_As
C_A0' = C_A0 - C_A0s
```

Substituting and simplifying gives a linear model of the form

```text
V dC_A'/dt = F C_A0' - (F + 2 V k C_As) C_A'
```

or

```text
tau dC_A'/dt + C_A' = K C_A0'
```

with

```text
tau = V / (F + 2 V k C_As)
K = F / (F + 2 V k C_As)
```

### What this example teaches

Linearization turns a hard nonlinear model into a simpler local model. The approximation is not exact, but it makes the effects of flow, kinetics, and operating point much easier to interpret.

## Linearization and Deviation Variables

A linearized model is most useful when written in deviation variables. Instead of predicting absolute values directly, it predicts how far the process moves away from a steady operating point.

This is helpful because control problems are usually phrased in terms of changes:

- a change in feed concentration
- a change in valve position
- a change in coolant flow
- a change in output temperature

For a linear model, the response to several small input changes can be added together. That superposition principle is one of the main reasons linear models are so useful in control.

Still, linearization is only valid near the operating point. If the disturbance is very large or the process is strongly nonlinear, the approximation may become poor.

## Numerical Solution of ODEs

When a model cannot be solved analytically, numerical integration is used.

The simplest method is Euler integration:

```text
y_(i+1) = y_i + f(y_i, t_i) Delta t
```

This method is easy to implement, but accuracy depends strongly on the step size.

A more accurate common method is fourth-order Runge-Kutta, which evaluates the derivative several times within each step. It usually gives much better accuracy for the same step size, though at higher computational cost.

The key numerical issue is step size:

- if `Delta t` is too large, the solution may be inaccurate
- if `Delta t` is too small, the computation may be inefficient

A useful rule is to choose the step size small relative to the smallest time constant in the system and then check whether reducing the step size further changes the answer significantly.

## Worked Example 4: One Euler Step

Take the first-order model

```text
dy/dt = -(1/tau) y + (K/tau) u
```

Suppose

- `tau = 5 min`
- `K = 2`
- `u = 3`
- `y(0) = 0`
- `Delta t = 1 min`

Then at `t = 0`

```text
dy/dt = -(1/5)(0) + (2/5)(3) = 1.2
```

Using Euler:

```text
y(1) ≈ y(0) + Delta t (dy/dt)
     = 0 + 1(1.2)
     = 1.2
```

So the estimated value after one minute is `1.2`.

This example is small, but it shows the numerical logic clearly: evaluate the slope, then step forward.

## A More Complex Dynamic Example: The Nonisothermal Reactor

A nonisothermal CSTR couples a material balance and an energy balance. Concentration and temperature influence each other through the reaction rate and heat of reaction. The result is much richer dynamic behavior than in simple first-order mixing problems.

Depending on kinetics, heat transfer, and operating conditions, the reactor may show:

- slow overdamped behavior
- fast but stable behavior
- oscillatory transients
- strong sensitivity to cooling changes

This is an important control lesson. Physical appearance alone does not reveal dynamic difficulty. A stirred tank can behave like a gentle first-order process or like a much more challenging oscillatory system depending on the physics inside it.

## Key Takeaways

Mathematical modelling in process control is a goal-oriented task. The model should be chosen to answer a specific engineering question with enough accuracy to support a decision.

Fundamental models are valuable because they connect process physics to dynamic behavior. They show how equipment design, operating conditions, and material properties influence response speed and steady-state effect.

Linearization is useful because it turns difficult nonlinear models into simpler local models with interpretable gains and time constants. Numerical methods are useful because many realistic models cannot be solved analytically.

The most important habit is to treat modelling as analysis, not just calculation. A good engineer does not stop after solving the equations. The engineer asks whether the result is physically meaningful, sensitive to uncertainty, and accurate enough for the problem being solved.
