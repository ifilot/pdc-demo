Process control begins with a practical observation: chemical plants do not sit still. Flows surge, inventories change, temperatures drift, and compositions move as disturbances enter the process. The role of control is to maintain desired conditions in this dynamic setting by adjusting selected variables in the plant.

A general feedback loop contains four essential pieces: a desired value or set point, a measurement of the current process condition, a control calculation, and a final element that acts on the process. In familiar examples such as automobile steering or room-temperature control, the same pattern appears again and again: measure, compare, decide, and act.

In process language, the output we want to keep near target is the controlled or measured variable, while the input adjusted by the controller is the manipulated variable. Feedback uses the measured output to influence the process input so that disturbances can be rejected without needing a perfect plant model.

The source chapters also make clear why control is necessary. Plants must respond both to disturbances and to changing desired values. In a heated tank, for example, inlet flow rate or feed temperature may shift unexpectedly, while production targets may require a new operating level or temperature. Control is what keeps the plant near acceptable operation as these changes occur.

These targets are not arbitrary. The opening objectives of process control are deeply operational: safety, environmental protection, equipment protection, smooth operation and production rate, product quality, profit, and monitoring and diagnosis. Good control supports all of them at once, rather than optimizing only a single variable.

$$\text{Control} = \text{maintain desired conditions by adjusting selected variables}$$
